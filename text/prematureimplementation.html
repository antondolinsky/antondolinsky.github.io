Premature Implementation

To some this will probably be obvious, but to me it came as a bit of a revelation.
When I was learning how to program and fevereshly devouring every scrap of information I could find about the field's fascinating history, I of course multiple times came upon the famous aphorism "Premature optimization is the root of all evil", attributed to Tony Hoare. But I realize now that for the longest time I did not understand its fuller meaning. I always approached it as a bit of advice about implementation. But now I see that it applies to all aspects of computing, including to its abstract and mathematically beautiful aspects, the place where programming is mathematics.
Premature optimization prevents one from thinking about programs in a purely mathematical way. I don't want to be misunderstood when I write about approaching programming mathematially... There is a whole tribe of functional programming devotees who seem to believe that thinking of programming in terms of stateless functions is the most mathematical way to approach the matter. This I disagree with. Functional programming offers many wonderful advantages, but it is not uniquely mathematical as a programming paradigm. Indeed, I suspect that singling out functional programming as being more mathematical than other paradigms is often a form of premature optimization. Two common arguments against state are that it is hard to reason about and makes compiler optimizations more difficult. But both of these are optimization matters. The first is concerned with optimizing the programmer's performance, the second with optimizing that of the machine.
In mathematics, performance issues don't matter — well, unless you want them to. Beauty and understanding can be pursued for their own sake. And it is precisely this attitude that can, I think, lead to the finest programs. Performance is, of course, extremely important — not only in code that one writes for money, but also in code that one writes for art and science's sake. Generally, when programming, one would like to see results on some sort of finite time frame. But performance optimization often should not be the first step. To consider performance at the beginning is to shut off entire branches of possibility.
In fact, it is not only a concern with performance that binds the programmer's hands. In the broader sense, all concern with implementation does — including the concern with a specific language. Obviously it is very fruitful to think in language, but to think in an existing language often imposes an unnecessary limitation. In the space of the mind, one can freely extend whatever language one likes to think in, and many of the best approaches emerge from a space that is beyond any particular language. Really, all progamming is language design. Whenever one codes, one is extending the language that one is coding in.
Let me interpret Hoare's statement broadly and say: "Premature implementation is the root of much evil". A thought experiment: imagine that one is programming for machines that are capable of arbitrarily fast code execution and have arbitrarily large memories. How does this affect one's idea of what sort of solutions are possible? This opens the mind to thinking mathematically. Then, once a solution comes to mind, one can consider performance matters. If the performance of the original solution is unacceptable, then one can revise. It is even possible that at some future point computers will appear that are capable of executing the original solution. Even if they do not, still - one will at least have contributed to the world's stock of mathematical understanding.
